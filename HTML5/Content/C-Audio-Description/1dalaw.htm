Audio Description and the Law

It is possible you might not have encountered audio description before today. This page will explain audio description's relationship to the law, including its spot in the Americans with Disabilities Act and the use of class-action lawsuits in the fight for proper accommodations.

What Laws Mandate Audio Description?

The laws that mandate audio description as a necessary accommodation are below:

Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act:
Federal agencies must make their electronic services accessible to disabled people. In 2001, the section was updated to include audio description as well: all film and video produced or procured by the federal government must include audio description.
21st Century Communications and Video Accessibility Act:
Passed by President Obama in 2010, this act mandates that, one year after the law's passing, the top four broadcast networks and top five cable channels in the 25 most populated markets (referred to in the law as designated market areas) must provide four hours of described audio per week. The law was written so that each year, its jurisdiction would incrementally expand until all designated market areas are required to provide audio description by law. This deadline is set in 2035.

Why is Described Audio so Scarce?

Despite it being mandatory for public entities, places of public accommodation, and all federal & federally funded programs (which includes many companies) to provide audio description, a majority of these entities are in noncompliance with this law. There are two main reasons for the scarcity of audio description: one, the noncompliance deadline is set to April 2027 (at the latest), and two, many of the people at these companies and in the government are simply not concerned with providing accommodations. Many companies are of the mindset that until required or asked, accessibility is not something that they have to think about. Often, people who need audio description or other services provided to them are left with very few options to ensure their needs are met. There are very few times that a company will provide a service unless it feels threatened by the force of the law, which is a part of the reason class-action suits against noncompliant companies are so prevalent.

The Class-Action Suit Against Hamilton

In 2017, a class-action lawsuit was brought against the Broadway show Hamilton for not providing proficient audio description and thus being in noncompliance with the Americans with Disabilities Act. In fact, very few Broadway shows offer audio description at all:

“A quick glance shows that most Broadway theaters are wheelchair accessible and offer open captioning and hearing assistance devices; there are also occasional special performances for people on the autism spectrum. But only four long-running musicals, including The Lion King and The Book of Mormon, offer audio description.” (Jeff Lunden, “A Blind Theatergoer's 'Hamilton' Lawsuit Aims Spotlight On Broadway Accessibility”)

It is important to note that the plaintiff was not seeking damages– under the Americans with Disabilities Act, they can’t. Many lawsuits such as the one above are often painted as extreme, and the plaintiffs in these cases are often accused of being money-hungry. But what critics fail to note is that lawsuits like these are often a last resort for disabled people, who live in a world that is and has always been hostile to them. Very few people have the money or time to sue. Most of the complaints filed in these types of suits, such as failure to provide audio description, are easily remedied by the defendant. So, most cases similar to the suit against Hamilton are not about money or personal gain at all. Instead, they are filed in order for people to get the accommodations they are owed, and to hopefully send a message to other noncompliant companies that they, too, can be held accountable for their noncompliance.
