Why Accessibility Matters

This page will explain why accessibility, particularly web accessibility, is imperative for everyone to not only be knowledgeable about, but to practice.

You Just Have to Care

There is no better argument for accessibility than this: disabled people are people, and all people should be able to access the web. A common tactic used when justifying accessibility features (most frequently employed by non-disabled people) is that accessibility benefits everyone. This is undeniably true. In fact, accessibility features benefit unintended audiences so much that the phenomenon has been dubbed the "curb cut" effect (since curb cuts benefit far larger groups of people than just those with mobility aids). But this argument detracts from the humanity of disabled people by basing their worth on whether or not non-disabled people will benefit from very necessary accommodations for disabled people, and it should not be used. The simple fact is that accessibility benefits those for whom the world is inaccessible, and that's all that needs to be said.

Perspectives on Accessibility

For further discussion on the importance of accessibility, and some important history on disability rights activism in the U.S., consider watching the videos linked below.

"Our fight for disability rights -- and why we're not done yet." A TED Talk by Judy Heumann.

The above video is a TED Talk given by the late Judy Heumann, one of the leaders of the disability rights movement in the 70s. In her talk, Heumann divulges some of her own personal history and some history of the disability rights movement in the United States (including the Capitol Crawl and the 504 Sit-In). She closes by stressing how far disabled people have come in the fight for equality, and how far left there is to go.

"What Accessibility Means to Me" by Juan Alcazar.

The above video is filmmaker Juan Alcazar's account of the importance of accessibility, particularly web accessibility, in his own life. In his video, Alcazar discusses the benefits of accessibility, how he benefits from web accessibility features, and he addresses some common misconceptions that non-disabled people might have about the importance of accessibility.
