What is Accessibility?

Though a seemingly easy question to answer, there is a lot of context behind what accessibility and disability actually are that are important to understand before diving into web accessibility. This page will explain the medical and social models of disability, along with defining accessibility and web accessibility.

The Medical vs. Social Model of Disability

When talking about accessibility, it is prudent to understand what is meant by disability. Historically, disability has been viewed through a medical lens, and thus was treated as a heavily individualized experience. Disability was viewed as something that can– and should be– cured. Disabled people were expected to adapt to the world around them, and although some concessions and gains have been made, the onus of accessibility lay on the disabled. This model of disability, also known as the medical model of disability, may seem outdated when presented in such a light. But in fact, the medical model of disability still informs an overwhelming majority of discussion about disability today, especially when the issue of accessibility is brought up.

There is, however, another emerging framework through which disability is discussed: the social model of disability. Understanding disability through a social model shifts the focus from disabled individuals onto society as a whole. The social model argues that inadequate accommodations for disabled people– inadequate accessibility– is what is disabling. To understand the social model better, let’s think of an example. How many people do you know that wear glasses? Do you yourself wear glasses? Vision impairment is a sensory disability (a disability related to a person’s ability to see, hear, or feel), yet very few people would consider someone who wears glasses disabled. This is because the disability has been accommodated for. It is routine for schoolchildren to have their vision checked, especially when they’re young. Glasses (or contacts) are readily available to many people. When accommodated properly, the disability has little impact on a person’s life at all.

So What Actually is Accessibility?

Accessibility can be defined rather simply: it is the degree to which an environment is usable by as many people as possible (Kalbag 3). For web accessibility, the environment in question is a website. But the way we talk about accessibility is inherently influenced by the way we talk about disability. If we are to follow the medical model of disability, we might be inclined to believe that disabled users should have their own tools and resources to make websites accessible– which many people do, in fact, use already (for example, screen readers). But by thinking about accessibility through the social model of disability, we enable ourselves to be active agents in accessible practice. Accessibility is not just an individual need or experience, it is a collective one.
